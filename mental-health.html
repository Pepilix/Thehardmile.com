Men's Mental Health · Yorkshire & North England

This is what men's mental health actually looks like.

Not a clinic. Not a helpline. A group of real men, doing something hard together — and talking about it after. The Hard Mile exists because young men need more than advice. They need a reason to show up.

The Problem

Men die by suicide at three times the rate of women. And most never ask for help.

In the UK, men account for around three quarters of all suicides. Suicide is the leading cause of death for men under 50. In Yorkshire and North England — where The Hard Mile is rooted — those numbers are no different.

The reason isn't that men don't struggle. It's that the traditional routes to help — therapy, talking to a GP, calling a helpline — don't fit how most men see themselves. From a young age, men are taught that needing help is weakness. So they don't ask. They internalise. They push on until they can't.

"40% of men have never spoken to anyone about their mental health — not a friend, not a family member, not a professional."

This is the problem The Hard Mile was built to address — not by repackaging the existing approach, but by creating something different entirely.

The Hard Mile Approach

Physical challenge as a vehicle for mental health.

The research is clear: men open up when they're doing something alongside each other — not when they're sitting face to face in a room designed for it. Shared effort, shared discomfort, shared achievement — these are the conditions under which men naturally begin to talk.

That's the foundation The Hard Mile is built on. We don't ask young men to come and talk about their feelings. We ask them to come and do something hard — and we create the space for honest conversation to happen naturally, after the effort.

01
The Physical Challenge

Hiking, trail running, cold water — a structured outdoor challenge hard enough to matter, safe enough to do together. Ages 16–25, Yorkshire and North England.

02
Phones Away. No Tech.

For the full day, phones are off. No social media, no noise, no performance. Just the group, the outdoors, and what's actually going on.

03
The Open Circle

After the physical effort, the group sits together. No agenda, no facilitator directing the conversation. Space to talk about life, pressure, goals — whatever's on your mind.

04
Earn the Badge

Every attendee earns an achievement patch and a personal reference letter from the support crew. Something real, something earned — that's the point.

05
A Brotherhood

Connection with other young men who showed up. A network built on shared effort — not shared circumstance.

This isn't therapy. It's not a clinical programme. It's a group of ordinary people who believe that the best thing you can do for a young man's mental health is give him something worth doing — and people worth doing it with.

Boys & Teenagers

Why boys' mental health starts earlier than most people realise.

Mental health problems in men rarely appear suddenly in adulthood. The patterns are set much earlier — in how teenage boys learn to respond to pressure, failure, and emotion. Boys who are taught that struggle is private, that asking for help is weakness, and that being tough means being silent — those are the young men who end up isolated in their twenties and thirties.

The Hard Mile works with young men aged 16–25 specifically because this is the window where those patterns can still change. Before they calcify. Before silence becomes a permanent setting.

For a teenager, turning up to a physical challenge with other young men their age — being pushed, earning something, and hearing that other people feel the same pressure they do — is genuinely life-changing. Not because of what's said. Because of what's experienced together.

What parents and schools should know

If you're a parent or teacher in Yorkshire or North England looking for mental health support for a young man aged 16–25, The Hard Mile community programme is a peer-led, outdoor, achievement-based alternative to traditional mental health services. It does not replace clinical care — but it reaches young men who would never walk through a clinic door.

The Bigger Picture

We don't just talk about it. We run 500km for it.

The Hard Mile community programme is only part of what this collective does. The other part is the endurance challenges — multi-day, physically extreme events that raise money for local charities including the Charlie Dallas Lancaster Foundation, a cause rooted in the very loss that makes men's mental health a personal mission for this team.

In 2025, Mark rowed 41 miles solo on an ergometer. Ben cycled and ran 500km from York to Brighton. In 2026, the full team ran a 500km relay from Ballater in Scotland to Thirsk in North Yorkshire — non-stop, 24 hours a day, for 60 hours.

These aren't sponsored athletes. They're business owners, managers, and working people who show up. Because showing up is the point.
